Visualizations for tracking progress — where this could go

Most health metrics — heart rate, blood pressure, weight — fold into a single number that simply moves up or down. Affect doesn't. Reducing a person's emotional life to a "happiness score" loses what actually matters: which feelings, how clearly named, how varied, and how they connect to events. Tracking emotional progress is more about pattern, vocabulary, and resonance than measurement.

That said, the affect-dynamics literature does converge on three operationally defined patterns that count as "progress" when tracked across many sessions:

  • Emotional inertia (Kuppens et al., 2010, Psychological Science) — the autocorrelation of affect across consecutive measurements. High inertia, especially in the presence of sustained negative valence, indexes a system that fails to respond to context, and is a robust correlate of depression and rumination. The clinical reading is "stuck", not "stable".
  • Affective resilience / recovery (Ong et al., 2006, Journal of Personality & Social Psychology) — the rate at which affect returns toward baseline after a perturbation. Faster, more reliable return is associated with better long-term adaptation; chronic non-recovery is a warning sign.
  • Emotional granularity (Barrett, 1998; Kashdan et al., 2015) — how finely a person discriminates among their own states. Operationally, the intraclass correlation among self-reported emotion ratings: high granularity means a person distinguishes "frustrated" from "anxious" from "overwhelmed"; low granularity collapses them into an undifferentiated bad mood. The construct opposite is alexithymia, not contentment. Distinct from affective variability (Gruber et al., 2013) — how much affect actually moves over time — and from expressive suppression (Gross, 1998, 2002), where a person experiences varied states internally but actively dampens their display. A label-driven instrument captures granularity directly; the other two are independent constructs that the captured data alone can't disambiguate.

The harder design question is which axis of the dimensional model (Russell, 1980; Mehrabian & Russell, 1974) to anchor longitudinal recipes on. There are three:

  • Valence — positive ↔ negative
  • Arousal — high ↔ low (energy / activation)
  • Dominance — in-control ↔ submissive (sometimes phrased as "out-of-control" in plain language)

Of these, valence is both the most intuitive for users (everyone reads positive vs. negative) and the dimension on which the field has the most settled operational definitions for the longitudinal dynamics patterns — specifically inertia (the autocorrelation of V across consecutive measurements) and resilience (V's rate of return toward baseline after a perturbation). Important nuance: the consensus is not that higher valence is "better" in any moral or therapeutic sense, but that V provides agreed operational definitions for these two dynamics measures specifically. Granularity is a different kind of construct — it is computed from the diversity and frequency of labels applied within a fixed window of time, not from V trajectories. So a future affect-kit recipe set might anchor inertia and resilience on V, while range/granularity reads the captured labels directly.

Arousal carries less interpretive consensus over time — the same elevated reading can index excitement, anxiety, or flow depending on context, and the literature has yet to settle on a clean longitudinal use. Dominance has very little experience-sampling research behind it; it appears in the original PAD model and in stimulus norming, but rarely as an outcome variable in dynamics work. affect-kit captures both dimensions in every Rating, but defers visualizing them until there is something defensible to claim.
