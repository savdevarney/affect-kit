Visualizations for tracking progress — where this could go

Most health metrics — heart rate, blood pressure, weight — fold into a single number that simply moves up or down. Affect doesn't. Reducing a person's emotional life to a "happiness score" loses what actually matters: which feelings, how clearly named, how varied, and how they connect to events. Tracking emotional progress is more about pattern, vocabulary, and resonance than measurement.

That said, the affect-dynamics literature does converge on three operationally defined patterns that count as "progress" when tracked across many sessions:

  • Inertia (Kuppens et al., 2010, Psychological Science) — the autocorrelation of affect across consecutive measurements. High inertia, especially in the presence of sustained negative valence, indexes a system that fails to respond to context, and is a robust correlate of depression and rumination. The clinical reading is "stuck", not "stable".
  • Resilience (Ong et al., 2006, Journal of Personality & Social Psychology) — the rate at which affect returns toward baseline after a perturbation. Faster, more reliable return is associated with better long-term adaptation; chronic non-recovery is a warning sign.
  • Range — colloquially, the breadth of someone's emotional repertoire. Two operational measures sit under this label; they capture related but distinct things, and both are worth tracking longitudinally:
    • Affective variability (Gruber et al., 2013) — how much affect actually moves across time, measured from the spread of V values over a window.
    • Emotional granularity (Barrett, 1998; Kashdan et al., 2015) — how finely a person discriminates among their own states. Operationally, the intraclass correlation across self-reported emotion ratings: high granularity means a person distinguishes "frustrated" from "anxious" from "overwhelmed"; low collapses them into an undifferentiated bad mood.
    A person can become more granular (gain vocabulary, learn to discriminate) without becoming more variable, and vice versa — both matter, for different reasons. The construct opposite, especially of granularity, is alexithymia — difficulty identifying or describing one's own feelings — not contentment. An adjacent regulation strategy is expressive suppression (Gross, 1998, 2002), the active dampening of emotional display.

The harder design question is which axis of the dimensional model (Russell, 1980; Mehrabian & Russell, 1974) to anchor longitudinal recipes on. There are three:

  • Valence — positive ↔ negative
  • Arousal — high ↔ low (energy / activation)
  • Dominance — in-control ↔ submissive (sometimes phrased as "out-of-control" in plain language)

Of these, valence is both the most intuitive for users (everyone reads positive vs. negative) and the dimension on which the field has the most settled operational definitions for the longitudinal patterns measured against V: inertia (autocorrelation of V), resilience (V's rate of return toward baseline), and the variability half of range (how much V moves across a window). Important nuance: the consensus is not that higher valence is "better" in any moral or therapeutic sense, but that V provides agreed operational definitions for these dynamics measures. Granularity — the other half of range — is a different kind of construct, computed from the diversity of labels applied within a window rather than from V trajectories. A future affect-kit recipe set might anchor inertia, resilience, and variability on V, while granularity reads the captured labels directly.

Arousal carries less interpretive consensus over time — the same elevated reading can index excitement, anxiety, or flow depending on context, and the literature has yet to settle on a clean longitudinal use. Dominance has very little experience-sampling research behind it; it appears in the original PAD model and in stimulus norming, but rarely as an outcome variable in dynamics work. affect-kit captures both dimensions in every Rating, but defers visualizing them until there is something defensible to claim.
